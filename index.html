What prevents people from making good decisions?

 

Uncertainty plays a major role and is driven by hidden information and luck.

We are built for tribes and often don't trust information from outside our tribe.

We are not built for disconfirming the things we already believe in.

Put the above together and things can really fall apart.

Motivated reasoning, tribes and practical ideas to avoid pitfalls

 

  • "Motivated reasoning" is a key problem!
  • Video below provides a great explanation of how we process / challenge info depending on whether it agrees with us or not.
  • Tribes give you a sense of belonging and distinctiveness (but also superiority...?)
  • Tribe is telling you what is true and trustworthy.
  • Beware of echo chambers! Danger is that even if you get great information from outside, it will be dismissed due to tribal problem and bias
  • How to avoid? Universalism. Whether it is Mussilini or your mom telling you the earth is round, it is still true!!!
  • Imagine information absent of the source. OR, imagine it being delivered from a source that you trust.

Thinking of decisions as bets

 

  • How does your thinking change if you think of something as a bet?
  • You retreat from a place of certainty to a place of probabilistic thinking.
  • Kind of questions you ask are now very different.
  • What would people with counterviews say about this?
  • We don’t have perfect information and the future is always uncertain.
  • Thinking like this makes one more open minded to why one might be wrong and makes you information hungry.
  • It also helps to refine beliefs and predictions.

Skill vs luck and self narritives...

 

  • Skill vs luck – the self serving bias is very strong. If I screw up vs if you screw up: the stories are different...
  • Form a good decision group with different values.
  • We don’t like a negative update to our self image…
  • Coaching example from a poker coach - are you just complaining or do you want to learn?
  • Reasoning to be accurate vs reasoning to be right.
  • Who wins in a bet – the person who is reasoning to be right or reasoning to be accurate?
  • Thinking of decisions as bets takes you from the right mode to the accurate mode
  • Three ideas to put in a group charter:
    • Focus and commitment to accuracy
    • Hold each other accountable
    • Be open to diverse viewpoints
  • Now one can process information differently, not through “luck vs smart” lense, but looking for mistakes/learnings/curiousity.
  • Also, what happens is that you get rewarded for saying “I think I made a mistake” and the learnings that happen from it.
  • Also, the effect this has on the tribal dynamic is interesting. Is your tribe interested in learning/improving or in moaning?

Key takeaways

 

  1. Think about the framing of our beliefs/decisions – from defending to the hilt to seeing it as provisional and subject to upgrade/improvement.
  2. Following from above, think of decisions as bets. Be information hungry / curious / seek counter views in order to be more accurate. From being right to being more accurate.
  3. Accept that evolution gets in our way and that even being armed with the information does not solve the problem. We need help and this means input / perspectives / challenge from others.
  4. Being honest and open about uncertainty creates the space for others to open up and share their views.
  5. Keep a focus on moving ahead from where you are – complaining is not helpful. Rather focus on seeking input / perspective / trying a different strategy.
  6. Be particularly mindful about “motivated reasoning” and how that gets amplified through the tribe effect. Consider how the group agrees to mitigate against this.
  7. Experiment with other techniques to improve decisions. For example deconstructing a decision beforehand, back-casting and pre-mortems
  8. Always see maintaining the status quo as a decision in own right.
  9. Be compassionate with yourself and others. This is always a work in progress, getting it wrong a little less or catching yourself sooner makes a huge difference.
  10. Know thyself – make Ulysses contracts for situations where you know you may experience specific challenges.
  11. Teams must challenge themselves to prevent / reduce “echo chamber” risk (ie just repeating the views the group all agrees are the truth).
  12. Even on hard to quantify decisions / beliefs, take a stab at a range and refine from there – this a healthy dynamic.
